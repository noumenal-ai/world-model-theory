The specification

“World model” becomes a checkable claim.

A representation E : S → Z is not a world model in isolation. It is a world model relative to three declarations: a target family 𝒯 (the quantities it is accountable for), a readout budget L (how a downstream head is allowed to read it), and a dynamics law P (how the environment evolves). Fix those, and the claim is decidable: change the targets or the budget and the answer can flip. The specification is the conjunction of three obligations, each of which we prove is an exact criterion: an impossibility on one side, a construction on the other.

Obligation 1 · Readout

The targets must remain readable.

Two states the targets tell apart, the encoder must tell apart. In the quantitative form, latent distance must price target gaps at the declared budget, and the bound runs both ways. A budget-L readout of a target exists exactly when the latent metric dominates that target’s gaps.

A transition law must exist at all.

The consequence for how models are claimed.

“This network is a world model” is not true or false until the target family and readout budget are named. Stated without them, it is unevaluable. Every result that follows is relative to a declaration, and several of them show precisely how the verdict moves as the declaration changes.

Where the specification bites

One obstruction governs every way a latent fails.

The obligations are not wishes; each is the flip side of a proved impossibility. A single lemma drives all of them: when an encoder is invariant under a transformation that moves a target, the target is gone from the latent for good, and no downstream head recovers it. Its quantitative form turns exact collapse into a distance bound, and its cleanest instances are read straight off machine-checked physics.

The mismatch lemma.

If the encoder treats a transformation b as noise (E ∘ b = E) while some target reads it as signal (T a ≠ T (b a)), then no readout g of the latent equals the target. The failure is total: it holds for readouts of any regularity, and it needs no probability, no architecture, and no training assumption.

E ∘ b = E  ∧  T a ≠ T (b a)  ⟹  ¬ ∃ g, T = g ∘ E

The same shape appears in evaluation: in a companion development, a planner that grades success by distance in its own latent reports near-perfect achievement (about 0.98) while its true progress to the goal barely tracks it (correlation about 0.30). That gap is the measurable shadow of a latent that scores itself in a geometry from which the goal-relevant direction has already been shed.

The calculus of objectives

An objective fixes what it can never see, before training starts.

Every training objective carries an invariance class: the transformations of the data whose effect it cannot register. That class is decided by the loss, the mask, and the guards: by the design, not by the optimizer, the scale, or the data. Anything inside it is invisible to every readout of the objective and to every statistical test built from it, so a proxy can pass in full while the quantity it is cited for sits provably out of reach. The theory turns this into an algebra: how invariance composes, how a mask narrows it, and how a matched objective repairs it, with the cost of repair stated exactly.

Law · Blindness

Invisible to the loss means invisible to every test.

If a transformation leaves the loss value unchanged, it also leaves unchanged every Boolean test computed from that loss, even after the data distribution is shifted by that very transformation. Passing such a proxy carries no evidence about the hidden quantity; the objective assigned it zero power in advance.

L θ (b a) m = L θ a m  ⟹  every loss-built test agrees on P and on b#P

Law · Masking

The mask decides the reach.

The transformations an objective cannot distinguish form a monoid, and a masked objective’s class is the intersection of the classes over its masks. Whatever a mask leaves unselected, the objective cannot police. This is a precise form of the intuition that a predictive mask both grants and bounds what a model is accountable for.

G(L) = ⊓ m G(L, m)  (a submonoid of transformations)

Law · Repair

One guard per broken symmetry, and no more.

A matched objective, prediction plus a guard for each symmetry it must not ignore, recovers the full invariance class. The separation is unconditional at the prediction/guard boundary; additional structure is required only to tell guards apart from one another, exactly where it is needed and nowhere else.

g ∈ G(matched)  ⟺  g ∈ G(pred) ∧ ∀ j, g ∈ G(guard j)

A new exact law: the exchange constant.

When two guard families act on a model under a shared symmetry, they could in principle mask each other’s response and hide a moved target between them. We prove the room for that is exactly one number: the guards can trade only a single shared constant, the data side up by c and the law side down by c. Require both families to respond monotonically and c collapses to zero: the families cannot cancel, and every broken symmetry that any guard names stays visible. The cancellation channel between guard families is one-dimensional, and we characterize it in full.

∃ c,  ∑ λ j ΔG j data = c  ∧  ∑ μ i ΔG i law = −c
mixed_guard_exchange

Read forward, this is a calculus for building. The same laws tell a builder which invariances are safe to impose, which erase a target, and which guard restores a broken symmetry, each guard packaged with a proof that it reads the structure it claims to read. A modeling goal becomes a typed list of obligations and repairs, with the algebra of the repairs settled in advance.

The forcing theorem

The admissible world model is unique, and the theory hands it to you.

The arguments so far rule representations out. This one is positive. Fix the three declarations, the targets 𝒯, the readout budget, and the dynamics P, and look at every way to compress the state space that keeps the targets and respects the transitions. We prove there is a single greatest such compression: the coarsest faithful representation, the bisimulation quotient of the state space, the partition of states into those that are genuinely different for prediction, the causal states. Not one option among many. The one the theory names. The construction assumes nothing about the domain: any state space, any target family, any transition law, with no finiteness and nothing physical required.

Result 1 · Canonicity

A single coarsest compression exists.

The compressions that satisfy the specification, those that refine the targets and respect the dynamics, are ordered by how much they keep. We prove their supremum is itself one of them, so there is a greatest element rather than a frontier of incomparable choices. This is the finite form of Larsen and Skou’s union-of-bisimulations theorem, carried by a single induction over the generated relation.

≈  =  ⨆ { r : r refines 𝒯 ∧ r respects P }  ∈  the same family

When a deterministic dynamics conserves every declared target, the forced latent is exactly the relation that holds when all the invariants agree, and the induced transition law is the identity, since a conserved quantity cannot move. The canonical world model of a conservative system is its chart of invariants under trivial dynamics, with latent space, encoder, and law all read off the declarations.

The forcing theorem, in one line.

The canonical quotient is spec-satisfying, it is the greatest such, and every world model refines it. The earlier arguments say which representations are impossible; this one says which representation is forced. What survives the specification is exactly the bisimulation quotient, and everything downstream, the geometry it carries and the architectures that realize it, is a theory of that single object.

Latent-state design becomes synthesis under declared obligations, in place of guessing what a network should preserve. The conserved case above is the interpretable flagship; for any domain that declares targets and transitions, the same construction returns the minimal latent state its role requires, and the levels that follow give its geometry and the architectures that realize it.

The geometry of the quotient

The canonical state space arrives with a canonical geometry.

The forcing theorem hands you a set of causal-state classes. This argument puts a distance on them, the Fisher–Rao distance between the one-step transition laws of two classes, and proves it measures exactly the behavioral difference the specification permits, and no more. The geometry exists because the quotient does, and it obeys the monotonicity law that separates a principled distance-between-distributions from a decorative one.

Result 1 · Existence

The metric costs nothing beyond the quotient.

The forced quotient already carries a well-defined transition law on its classes, so comparing two classes by the Fisher–Rao distance between their laws is at once a genuine function of the classes, with no new well-definedness to discharge. It is zero on the diagonal and symmetric. The geometry falls out of the same lift that produced the classes.

bisimMetric c c’ = dFR(P c, P c’)  ·  d(c, c) = 0,  d(c, c’) = d(c’, c)

Result 2 · Pricing

Distance bounds every test that could tell classes apart.

For any Boolean test on classes, the gap between its expectations under the two classes’ transition laws is at most the sine of half their distance. One number bounds one-step behavioral distinguishability across all tests at once, and bisimilar states, at distance zero, have identical test power. The metric prices exactly the differences the specification lets classes have.

| EP c[f] − EP c’[f] |  ≤  sin( dFR(c, c’) / 2 )  for every Boolean f

Result 3 · Monotonicity

Forgetting information never manufactures distance.

Push both transition laws through any map and the Fisher–Rao distance cannot increase: the overlap between two distributions only grows under coarse-graining. This is the finite Chentsov monotonicity, the data-processing inequality for this geometry: you cannot conjure behavioral distinguishability by discarding detail, which is what makes a distance built from probabilities trustworthy.

dFR(map g p, map g q)  ≤  dFR(p, q)  for every g

One direction here is open, and we mark it as a conjecture. Everything above is proved. Whether this geometry is the only one the specification admits, whether any distance that is natural under congruent embeddings and monotone under coarse-graining must agree with Fisher–Rao up to scale (a finite Chentsov uniqueness), and whether it is the one-step layer of the Ferns discounted fixed point, is a precise conjecture we are proving next. The monotonicity above is its first proved step.

Architectures for physics

Which prediction horizons an architecture can support is fixed by the physics.

A latent transition law does not exist at every time-horizon just because you want it to. For a fixed encoder and a fixed flow, the horizons at which a latent step exists form an algebraic object, a subset of time, and its size is an invariant of the representation, not a training outcome. Read across the physics, that invariant takes exactly three settings: {0} for free kinematics, the half-period lattice (π/ω)ℤ for the oscillator, and all of time for a representation that keeps what the dynamics actually distinguishes. One law; three classes.

The realizable horizons form a group, once the world is finite.

For any encoder against any flow, the horizons that admit a latent dynamics are closed under zero and addition: they are a submonoid of time. When the state space is finite they close under negation as well, since a pigeonhole period turns any backward step into a forward one, so they form a subgroup. Finiteness earns that closure: on an infinite state space we exhibit an encoder whose admissible horizons contain +1 but not −1, a submonoid that is genuinely not a group. The algebra of realizable horizons is pinned down exactly.

The executable layer

The impossibility runs on real floating-point, and its input is a theorem.

A formal result about an idealized real-valued network leaves a gap: the network that actually runs is floating-point. This layer closes that gap for the readout impossibility. Because a float32 value is a dyadic rational, its exact real value is computable inside the proof kernel, so the quantity you would normally obtain by running the encoder is instead proved, exactly, and the impossibility theorem fires on the literal executed computation with no idealization in between.

The empirical antecedent is computed, not assumed.

Take two adjacent binary32 values and make the latents their exact decodes. The gap between those latents, the empirical fact a readout bound depends on, is then a decidable rational, settled by decide on the bit-fields and norm_num on the arithmetic. No native_decide, no opaque Float, no added axioms: the measurement that would normally be reported from a run is a kernel-checked equation.

No Lipschitz reading of the executed encoder recovers the target.

A target separates the two samples by a unit gap, which exceeds the budget-scaled latent gap 1 · 2⁻²³. The McShane impossibility then applies with every antecedent kernel-proven: no 1-Lipschitz reading of the executed encoder computes the target. The boundary of Argument 1 holds on the real computation itself, not on an idealization of it.

The scope is exactly what is claimed. The executed map is minimal, two decoded bit-patterns, and the target is a stipulated unit-gap labelling, so the empirical content here is entirely on the encoder side. What the layer establishes is the method: that an impossibility whose antecedent is an executed floating-point quantity can be discharged in-kernel, exactly, with no native_decide and no opaque floats. Scaling the latents to a literal executed attention head and grounding the target is the next step, and the bridge that makes it feasible, that float32 is dyadic, is already the load-bearing lemma here.

What the theory is for

A standard for building world models, and for judging them.

Read forward, the seven levels are an engineering discipline: declare what a model is for, build the representation the forcing theorem selects, inherit its geometry and its prediction horizons, and read it within a stated budget, each step a theorem rather than a heuristic. Read as an evaluation standard, the same theory is sharp: a proxy objective that passes carries no evidence about a quantity it is structurally blind to. This is not another architecture to set beside the predictive, generative, and agentic ones. It is the specification they are all measured against: declare the targets and the budget, and one grammar says what a model must preserve, checks whether it does, and names the guard that repairs it. It applies wherever a latent is claimed to stand in for an external world, from robotics and driving to scientific modeling and agent memory. The clearest worked case is the family of latent self-supervised models, the JEPA-class systems that predict in representation space.

The JEPA boundary.

A JEPA predictor is, by construction, a function of the context representation alone. So it can see only what the context-encoder keeps: if the encoder sends two inputs with different true targets to the same context, then no predictor-and-decoder pair recovers that target at both inputs, and the represented target is simply not a function of the JEPA context. This is the mismatch lemma of Argument 2, placed at the JEPA architecture and stated against the real objective, not a stand-in for it.

The challenge, and we hold it.

A JEPA-class system is not a world model for a target its encoder collapses. The boundary above is proved and unconditional: the predictor reads the context alone, so a collapsed target is unrecoverable by any predictor and any decoder, before a single step of training. This is the generic situation, not a corner case; it applies to every target the objective does not force the encoder to keep. The boundary is proved, and the collapse is also measured. A latent-predictive planner grading success in its own representation reports near-perfect achievement (about 0.98) while its correlation with true goal attainment stays near 0.30, and we have audited JEPA-class systems whose own success reports do not survive scrutiny. So the burden sits with the claimant: name the target family and the readout budget, and exhibit the guard that keeps them. Absent that guard the boundary applies, and we have tested it enough to say so. The theory even names the guard: the forced representation of Argument 4 and the matched objective of Argument 3.

The reach is general. The self-grading failure is not particular to planning. Whenever a system reads its success from the same latent that discarded a task-relevant quantity, it can pass its own test while failing the world. Latent evaluation is not external verification, and the specification is what external verification requires.

The claim

“World model” is not a property a network has. It is a relation among three things you must declare: a representation, the family of targets it has to serve, and the readout budget you allow. Declare them, and the mathematics leaves exactly one representation standing: the bisimulation quotient, the system’s causal states for that purpose, and every world model factors through it.
