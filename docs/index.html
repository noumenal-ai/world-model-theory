A representation is a world model only when the mathematics says it is.

We give the phrase world model a definition that can be checked. Declare what a latent is for — the target quantities, the readout budget, and the dynamics it must respect — and the theory determines which distinctions the latent must keep, which transitions can descend to it, and which representation is forced. Every claim on this site is a theorem in Lean 4, checked by the proof kernel, with the axioms it rests on named in full.

The specification

“World model” becomes a checkable claim.

A representation E : S → Z is not a world model in isolation. It is a world model relative to three declarations: a target family 𝒯 (the quantities it is accountable for), a readout budget L (how a downstream head is allowed to read it), and a dynamics law P (how the environment evolves). Fix those, and the claim is decidable: change the targets or the budget and the answer can flip. The specification is the conjunction of three obligations, each of which we prove is an exact criterion — an impossibility on one side, a construction on the other.

Obligation 1 · Readout

The targets must remain readable.

Two states the targets tell apart, the encoder must tell apart. In the quantitative form, latent distance must price target gaps at the declared budget — and this is not merely necessary. A budget-L readout of a target exists exactly when the latent metric dominates that target’s gaps.

|T s₁ − T s₂| ≤ L · d(E s₁, E s₂)  ⟺  ∃ g, LipschitzWith L g ∧ T = g ∘ E

Obligation 2 · Dynamics

A transition law must exist at all.

The encoder’s fibers must be a congruence for the dynamics. If two states map to the same latent but evolve to distinguishable futures, no map on the latent space reproduces the environment’s step — the representation has no well-defined transition law, however useful it looks under a proxy objective.

Obligation 3 · Quotient

The admissible representation is forced.

Among all representations meeting the first two obligations, there is a greatest one: the coarsest conforming quotient, which the theory computes as the bisimulation quotient of the state space relative to the declared targets and dynamics. A world model does not choose an arbitrary hidden vector; it refines this canonical object.

The consequence for how models are claimed.

“This network is a world model” is not true or false until the target family and readout budget are named. Stated without them, it is unevaluable. Every result that follows is relative to a declaration, and several of them show precisely how the verdict moves as the declaration changes.

Where the specification bites

One obstruction governs every way a latent fails.

The obligations are not wishes; each is the flip side of a proved impossibility. A single lemma drives all of them: when an encoder is invariant under a transformation that moves a target, the target is gone from the latent for good, and no downstream head recovers it. Its quantitative form turns exact collapse into a distance bound, and its cleanest instances are read off machine-checked physics, not toy constructions.

The mismatch lemma.

If the encoder treats a transformation b as noise (E ∘ b = E) while some target reads it as signal (T a ≠ T (b a)), then no readout g of the latent equals the target. The failure is total: it holds for readouts of any regularity, and it needs no probability, no architecture, and no training assumption.

E ∘ b = E  ∧  T a ≠ T (b a)  ⟹  ¬ ∃ g, T = g ∘ E

Instance · Conservation

A conserved quantity becomes unrecoverable.

Take the harmonic oscillator’s energy — conserved by a theorem, not by assumption. A position-only latent identifies two states of equal position and different velocity. Their energies differ, so no function of that latent computes the energy of the motion.

E⟨x₀, 0⟩ = E⟨x₀, v⟩,  energy differs  ⟹  energy is not readable

Instance · Geometry

Physics sets a floor on latent distance.

If a latent is to admit even an L-Lipschitz reading of that conserved energy, its geometry must pay for it: the two collapsed states must sit apart in latent space by at least the energy gap over the budget. Faithful representation is priced by the physics.

Instance · Dynamics

No transition law off the sampling times.

For the same latent under the true oscillator flow, a latent transition map exists only at half-period horizons; at every other horizon there is none. A model that looks predictive because it was sampled on the beat has, in fact, no dynamics law between the beats.

The physics is not decorative. Every instance above is stated against a fully formalized harmonic oscillator, with the conservation law and the solution flow proved in the kernel — so “the latent loses energy” is a theorem about the genuine physical quantity, not a themed analogy. The same shape appears in evaluation: in a companion development, a planner that grades success by distance in its own latent reports near-perfect achievement (about 0.98) while its true progress to the goal barely tracks it (correlation about 0.30) — the measurable shadow of a latent that scores itself in a geometry from which the goal-relevant direction has already been shed.

The calculus of objectives

An objective fixes what it can never see, before training starts.

Every training objective carries an invariance class: the transformations of the data whose effect it cannot register. That class is decided by the loss, the mask, and the guards — by the design, not the optimizer, the scale, or the data. Anything inside it is invisible to every readout of the objective and to every statistical test built from it, so a proxy can pass in full while the quantity it is cited for sits provably out of reach. The theory turns this into an algebra: how invariance composes, how a mask narrows it, and how a matched objective repairs it, with the cost of repair stated exactly.

Law · Blindness

Invisible to the loss means invisible to every test.

If a transformation leaves the loss value unchanged, it also leaves unchanged every Boolean test computed from that loss — even after the data distribution is shifted by that very transformation. Passing such a proxy carries no evidence about the hidden quantity; the objective assigned it zero power in advance.

L θ (b a) m = L θ a m  ⟹  every loss-built test agrees on P and on b#P

Law · Masking

The mask decides the reach.

The transformations an objective cannot distinguish form a monoid, and a masked objective’s class is the intersection of the classes over its masks. Whatever a mask leaves unselected, the objective cannot police — a precise form of the intuition that a predictive mask both grants and bounds what a model is accountable for.

G(L) = ⊓ m G(L, m)  (a submonoid of transformations)

Law · Repair

One guard per broken symmetry, and no more.

A matched objective — prediction plus a guard for each symmetry it must not ignore — recovers the full invariance class. The separation is unconditional at the prediction/guard boundary; additional structure is required only to tell guards apart from one another, exactly where it is needed and nowhere else.

g ∈ G(matched)  ⟺  g ∈ G(pred) ∧ ∀ j, g ∈ G(guard j)

A new exact law: the exchange constant.

When two guard families act on a model under a shared symmetry, they could in principle mask each other’s response and hide a moved target between them. We prove the room for that is exactly one number: the guards can trade only a single shared constant, the data side up by c and the law side down by c. Require both families to respond monotonically and c collapses to zero — the families cannot cancel, and every broken symmetry that any guard names stays visible. The cancellation channel between guard families is one-dimensional, and we characterize it in full.

∃ c,  ∑ λ j ΔG j data = c  ∧  ∑ μ i ΔG i law = −c
mixed_guard_exchange

Read forward, this is a calculus, not a critique. The same laws tell a builder which invariances are safe to impose, which erase a target, and which guard restores a broken symmetry — each guard packaged with a proof that it reads the structure it claims to read. A modeling goal becomes a typed list of obligations and repairs, with the algebra of the repairs settled in advance.

The forcing theorem

The admissible world model is unique, and the theory hands it to you.

The arguments so far rule representations out. This one is positive. Fix the three declarations — the targets 𝒯, the readout budget, the dynamics P — and look at every way to compress the state space that keeps the targets and respects the transitions. We prove there is a single greatest such compression: the coarsest faithful representation, the bisimulation quotient of the state space — the partition of states into those that are genuinely different for prediction, the causal states. Not one option among many. The one the theory names.

Result 1 · Canonicity

A single coarsest compression exists.

The compressions that satisfy the specification — refine the targets, respect the dynamics — are ordered by how much they keep. We prove their supremum is itself one of them, so there is a greatest element rather than a frontier of incomparable choices. This is the finite form of Larsen and Skou’s union-of-bisimulations theorem, carried by a single induction over the generated relation.

≈  =  ⨆ { r : r refines 𝒯 ∧ r respects P }  ∈  the same family

Result 2 · Descent

The dynamics descends for free.

On the canonical quotient every declared target is still defined, and the environment’s transition law pushes forward to a transition law on the classes — with a commuting square that holds by definition, not as a side condition to be discharged. The latent dynamics Obligation 2 demanded exists here by construction. Nothing has to be fit.

Result 3 · Universality

Every world model already computes it.

Any representation that satisfies the specification refines the canonical quotient, and the causal-state classification factors through it — whatever hidden vector the model carries, one fixed map reads the causal states back out of it. Two systems built independently, on unrelated latent spaces, compute the same object underneath. The quotient is not a construction we impose on a model; it is what every valid model was already doing.

The forcing theorem, in one line.

The canonical quotient is spec-satisfying, it is the greatest such, and every world model refines it. The earlier arguments say which representations are impossible; this one says which representation is forced. What survives the specification is exactly the bisimulation quotient, and everything downstream — the geometry it carries, the architectures that realize it — is a theory of that single object.

forcing_theorem le_bisimilarity

This is a build order, not a taxonomy. Once the targets, the budget, and the dynamics are declared, the representation is fixed up to relabeling. A team does not search a space of candidate encoders and hope one is faithful; the theory hands them the object to aim for, and the remaining work is to realize it in a trainable architecture — which is the subject of the levels that follow.

The geometry of the quotient

The canonical state space arrives with a canonical geometry.

The forcing theorem hands you a set of causal-state classes. This argument puts a distance on them — the Fisher–Rao distance between the one-step transition laws of two classes — and proves it measures exactly the behavioral difference the specification permits, and no more. The geometry is not an add-on chosen for convenience: it exists because the quotient does, and it obeys the monotonicity law that separates an honest distance-between-distributions from a decorative one.

Result 1 · Existence

The metric costs nothing beyond the quotient.

The forced quotient already carries a well-defined transition law on its classes, so comparing two classes by the Fisher–Rao distance between their laws is at once a genuine function of the classes — no new well-definedness to discharge. It is zero on the diagonal and symmetric. The geometry falls out of the same lift that produced the classes.

bisimMetric c c’ = dFR(P c, P c’)  ·  d(c, c) = 0,  d(c, c’) = d(c’, c)
bisimMetric bisimMetric_self bisimMetric_comm
Result 2 · Pricing

Distance bounds every test that could tell classes apart.

For any Boolean test on classes, the gap between its expectations under the two classes’ transition laws is at most the sine of half their distance. One number bounds one-step behavioral distinguishability across all tests at once — and bisimilar states, at distance zero, have identical test power. The metric prices exactly the differences the specification lets classes have.

| EP c[f] − EP c’[f] |  ≤  sin( dFR(c, c’) / 2 )  for every Boolean f
bisimMetric_prices_tests bisim_zero_one_step_power finite_fisherRao_risk_bound
Result 3 · Monotonicity

Forgetting information never manufactures distance.

Push both transition laws through any map and the Fisher–Rao distance cannot increase — the overlap between two distributions only grows under coarse-graining. This is the finite Chentsov monotonicity, the data-processing inequality for this geometry: you cannot conjure behavioral distinguishability by discarding detail, which is what makes a distance built from probabilities trustworthy.

dFR(map g p, map g q)  ≤  dFR(p, q)  for every g

One direction here is open, and we state it as a conjecture, not a result. Everything above is proved. Whether this geometry is the only one the specification admits — whether any distance that is natural under congruent embeddings and monotone under coarse-graining must agree with Fisher–Rao up to scale (a finite Chentsov uniqueness), and whether it is the one-step layer of the Ferns discounted fixed point — is a precise conjecture we are proving next. The monotonicity above is its first proved step.

Architectures for physics

Which prediction horizons an architecture can support is fixed by the physics.

A latent transition law does not exist at every time-horizon just because you want it to. For a fixed encoder and a fixed flow, the horizons at which a latent step exists form an algebraic object — a subset of time — and its size is an invariant of the representation, not a training outcome. Read across the physics, that invariant takes exactly three settings: {0} for free kinematics, the half-period lattice (π/ω)ℤ for the oscillator, and all of time for a representation that keeps what the dynamics actually distinguishes. One law; three classes.

The realizable horizons form a group — once the world is finite.

For any encoder against any flow, the horizons that admit a latent dynamics are closed under zero and addition: they are a submonoid of time. When the state space is finite they close under negation as well — a pigeonhole period turns any backward step into a forward one — so they form a subgroup. Finiteness earns that closure: on an infinite state space we exhibit an encoder whose admissible horizons contain +1 but not −1, a submonoid that is genuinely not a group. The algebra of realizable horizons is pinned down exactly.

admissibleHorizons E : AddSubmonoid T  — an AddSubgroup when S is finite;  on ℤ:  +1 ∈,  −1 ∉

Spectrum · Kinematics

Free motion, position only: nothing but the trivial horizon.

Take a free particle and a latent that keeps position and drops velocity. The admissible set is exactly {0}: no nonzero horizon carries a latent dynamics. A representation that has shed velocity cannot be repaired by sampling, at any rate — the bottom of the spectrum is the horizon that does nothing.

Spectrum · Oscillator

The harmonic oscillator: the half-period lattice.

For the true oscillator flow with the same position-only latent, a latent step exists at exactly the integer multiples of the half-period — the stroboscopic lattice (π/ω)ℤ. Between the beats there is no transition law; on them the flow aliases to a scalar on position. A nontrivial subgroup, but a thin one: the middle of the spectrum, read off PhysLean’s literal solution flow.

Spectrum · Forced quotient

The representation the theory selects: every horizon at once.

One dichotomy governs all three cases: a horizon is admissible exactly when the flowed reading factors through the encoder. For a lossy latent it fails almost everywhere; for the representation the forcing theorem selects — the one whose fibers respect the dynamics — it holds at every horizon, so the admissible set is all of time. The top of the spectrum is what faithfulness buys.

t ∈ admissibleHorizons E  ⟺  (s ↦ E (t +ᵥ s)) factors through E

The spectrum runs, not just proves. The oscillator’s half-period aliasing is realized as blockModel, a type-checked flow world model on a six-state cycle, and its dichotomy is settled by the kernel on the literal finite dynamics — horizon 3 admits a latent step (three_mem_block), horizon 1 does not (one_not_mem_block). And in that architecture, admissibility of every declared horizon is a derived theorem (FlowWorldModel.horizons_admissible), never a field you get to assert: the flow-correctness certificate cannot be made circular.

The executable layer

The impossibility runs on real floating-point, and its input is a theorem.

A formal result about an idealized real-valued network leaves a gap: the network that actually runs is floating-point. This layer closes that gap for the readout impossibility. Because a float32 value is a dyadic rational, its exact real value is computable inside the proof kernel — so the quantity you would normally obtain by running the encoder is instead proved, exactly, and the impossibility theorem fires on the literal executed computation with no idealization in between.

The empirical antecedent is computed, not assumed.

Take two adjacent binary32 values and make the latents their exact decodes. The gap between those latents — the empirical fact a readout bound depends on — is then a decidable rational, settled by decide on the bit-fields and norm_num on the arithmetic. No native_decide, no opaque Float, no added axioms: the measurement that would normally be reported from a run is a kernel-checked equation.

E false = 1,  E true = 1 + 2⁻²³  ⟹  dist(E false, E true) = 2⁻²³

Layer · Antecedent

The latent gap is one unit in the last place.

The distance between the two executed latents is exactly 2⁻²³ — a single ulp, read straight off the decodes. The latents are distinct, so the collapse is genuinely approximate rather than exact: the impossibility that follows is the quantitative, distance-bounded one, not the degenerate zero-gap case.

Layer · Impossibility

No Lipschitz reading of the executed encoder recovers the target.

A target separates the two samples by a unit gap, which exceeds the budget-scaled latent gap 1 · 2⁻²³. The McShane impossibility then applies with every antecedent kernel-proven: no 1-Lipschitz reading of the executed encoder computes the target. The boundary of Argument 1 holds on the real computation, not only on the idealization of it.

|T false − T true| = 1  >  L · 2⁻²³  ⟹  ¬ ∃ g, LipschitzWith 1 g ∧ T = g ∘ E

The scope is exactly what is claimed. The executed map is minimal — two decoded bit-patterns — and the target is a stipulated unit-gap labelling, so the empirical content here is entirely on the encoder side. What the layer establishes is the method: that an impossibility whose antecedent is an executed floating-point quantity can be discharged in-kernel, exactly, with no native_decide and no opaque floats. Scaling the latents to a literal executed attention head and grounding the target is the next step, and the bridge that makes it feasible — float32 is dyadic — is already the load-bearing lemma here.

What the theory is for

A standard for building world models, and for judging them.

Read forward, the seven levels are an engineering discipline: declare what a model is for, build the representation the forcing theorem selects, inherit its geometry and its prediction horizons, and read it within a stated budget — each step a theorem rather than a heuristic. Read as an evaluation standard, the same theory is sharp: a proxy objective that passes carries no evidence about a quantity it is structurally blind to. The clearest worked case is the family of latent self-supervised models — JEPA-class systems that predict in representation space.

The JEPA boundary.

A JEPA predictor is, by construction, a function of the context representation alone. So it can see only what the context-encoder keeps: if the encoder sends two inputs with different true targets to the same context, then no predictor-and-decoder pair recovers that target at both inputs — the represented target is simply not a function of the JEPA context. This is the mismatch lemma of Argument 2, placed at the JEPA architecture, and it is stated against the real objective rather than a stand-in.

E i₁ = E i₂  ∧  T i₁ ≠ T i₂  ⟹  ¬ ∃ dec, ∀ x, T x = dec (predict (E x) k)

Boundary · Architecture

The predictor sees only what the encoder kept.

The JEPA predictor has the shape predict : Context → index → Pred: it reads the context at a target index and nothing more. Composing any decoder onto it is therefore a readout of the context alone, so it inherits whatever the context-encoder discarded. The boundary is a consequence of the architecture, settled before any training run.

The scope is stated precisely. The dissociation this predicts is the one Argument 2 quantified: a latent planner grading success in its own representation can report near-perfect achievement while its correlation with true goal attainment stays low. The theory names the condition under which that gap is forced, and the representation that closes it.
